# impeccable

Design context for `deck`. Loaded by the `/impeccable` skill before any design work so output stays grounded and consistent across sessions.

## Design Context

### Users

Jin (the author) is the primary and effectively the only user: a senior engineer driving Claude Code agent sessions and tmux shells from one machine over a tailnet. Secondary users are developers who clone the MIT repo and self-host their own copy.

Context of use is hands-on and frequent, often for long stretches, across desktop and an installed phone PWA. The user is always here to *do work in a session*, never to admire the shell around it. The job to be done: see every session at a glance, jump into one, and read or steer its output (agent transcript, line diffs, terminal grid) with zero friction. The interface is plumbing for someone who reads diffs and command output all day.

### Brand Personality

Quiet, precise, trustworthy. Three words: **calm, exact, unobtrusive.**

deck is an *invisible instrument*. It should feel like well-made professional equipment, the kind you stop noticing because nothing about it asks for attention, yet everything is where your hand expects it. The identity lives in restraint and precision, not in expression. No personality flourishes, no mascot energy, no decoration that competes with session content. If a choice makes the chrome more noticeable than the transcript, it is wrong.

Emotional goal: control and calm. The user should feel the tool is fast, solid, and out of the way.

### Aesthetic Direction

Restrained, content-first, dark-first. Ambition is high on *craft* and low on *flash*: build a strong, deliberate identity out of typographic rhythm, a disciplined palette, exact spacing, and optical detail, all in service of disappearing.

- **Reference (feel, not look-copying):** Vercel / Geist. Sharp, neutral, technically restrained, every element earning its place. The craft is invisible until you look closely; deck should read as that kind of precise, unshowy tool.
- **Colour direction.** Use the brand palette (see Brand colours below): a hot orange hero and a deep desaturated teal secondary over tinted near-neutrals. Brand presence comes through tiny, deliberate hits of orange, not field after field of it.
- **Anti-references:** the AI-slop palette (neon cyan-on-dark, purple/blue gradients, glow), glassmorphism, gradient text, decorative side-stripe borders, oversized rounded icon-tiles above every heading, dashboards full of identical cards. Also avoid "consumer SaaS marketing page" energy, this is a tool, not a landing page. Note: the teal is deliberately deep and desaturated, never let it drift toward neon cyan, and never gradient orange into teal.
- **Theme:** **dark is home.** Design dark-first, then derive light so it holds up, then verify e-ink. All three are real, but dark is where the user lives.
- **Identity is daisyUI-optional:** daisyUI v5 is underneath for speed, but it is scaffolding, not law. Own the palette and type scale; override or drop daisyUI tokens and components wherever they fight the vision. Don't ship the stock daisyUI look as the destination.

### Hard Constraints (from the codebase, non-negotiable)

- **Three themes:** `light` (default), `dark` (prefers-dark), `eink`. Every design must work in all three.
- **E-ink is a real reading mode, not a gimmick.** Under `[data-theme='eink']`: no motion, no transition, no box-shadow, no text-shadow (already globally enforced), and captured terminal colour is stripped to monochrome. Therefore: **never let colour, depth, or motion be the only carrier of meaning.** State and hierarchy must survive in pure monochrome via weight, size, border, and layout. Test every component mentally in e-ink.
- **Typography is system sans, no web-font download.** Keep the UI on a system font stack on purpose (speed, zero payload). Identity comes from scale, weight, spacing, and colour, *not* from a typeface. The single exception is the bundled `Hack Nerd Font Mono`, which exists solely for **shell rendering**: captured tmux grids, command output, prompt glyphs (powerline/git), and code, where monospacing and the Nerd Font glyphs are functionally required. Do not press it into UI chrome, and do not introduce a UI web font without a deliberate decision to revisit this. A small tracking-tight detail (around -0.02em on headings) is fine since it costs nothing.
- **Icons:** Lucide (`@lucide/svelte`) only, imported as components. No inline `<svg>`, no emoji anywhere in UI copy or labels.
- **Density:** the app is intentionally compact (small controls, joins, badges, recency-sorted flat lists). Keep that density, but earn calm through spacing rhythm and clear hierarchy so it reads composed, not cramped.
- **Shape and depth: slightly soft, always flat.** Light and dark use a small corner radius (about 4 to 6px) on controls and cards, a touch tighter than the brand's 8px to stay crisp. No shadows in any theme: separate surfaces with 1px hairline borders and stepped background tints (base-100 / 200 / 300), not elevation. e-ink already forces radius 0 and zero depth, so a flat, border-led system is the only one that survives all three themes. Set daisyUI `--depth: 0` and lean on borders.
- **Accessibility: WCAG AA, strict, in all three themes.** Text and meaningful UI meet AA contrast (4.5:1 body text, 3:1 large text and UI / non-text elements) in light, dark, and e-ink. Verify orange and teal at the sizes they are actually used: small orange text on dark often fails AA, so prefer orange as a mark or fill, bump weight/size, or pair it with a neutral label. Honour `prefers-reduced-motion` by dropping non-essential motion, on top of e-ink's blanket motion kill. Never encode state by hue alone (this also covers colour-blindness; orange/teal is a reasonable CB pair, but error-red versus running-orange must differ by more than colour).
- **Responsive: mobile-first, desktop earns its width.** deck is an installed phone PWA first, so design for the phone first: a single recency-sorted column, thumb-reachable primary actions, drawer/hamburger nav, full-width controls, safe-area aware. Then *use* the extra desktop space rather than centring one narrow column on a wide screen: a persistent project-grouped sidebar, multi-column or wider denser layouts, more visible at once, and keyboard affordances. Adapt the interface to the context, never just shrink or stretch it, and never amputate functionality on mobile. Prefer container queries for components and viewport queries for page layout.

### Design Principles

1. **Content is the interface.** Chrome recedes; the agent transcript, the diff, and the terminal grid are the subject. When in doubt, remove the UI element rather than style it.
2. **Identity through precision, not decoration.** With a system font and a tight palette, every gram of quality comes from rhythm, alignment, optical sizing, and consistency. Sweat those; add nothing ornamental.
3. **Survive e-ink.** If a state, status, or hierarchy reads only because of colour, depth, or motion, it is broken. Encode meaning structurally first, then enhance with colour/motion for the screen themes.
4. **Dark-first, all-three-true.** Make it excellent in dark, correct in light, legible in e-ink. None of the three may look like an afterthought.
5. **Calm density.** Power-tool compactness with breathing room and a real spatial scale. Tight groupings, generous separations between groups, never uniform padding everywhere.

### Brand colours

The implemented theme tokens live in `src/routes/layout.css`. Two brand colours over near-neutral surfaces:

- **Signal orange** `#FE491A` (the hero / primary). Approx `oklch(66% 0.21 36)`. A hot, red-leaning orange.
- **Slate teal** `#1F5B65` (secondary). Approx `oklch(42% 0.055 213)`. Deep, desaturated, calm.
- Neutrals on the site are effectively grayscale; deck should tint them slightly toward the teal hue (see below) so the palette feels of-a-piece rather than orange-on-gray.

Role mapping for deck (dark-first):

- **Orange = the signature accent (the one accent).** It owns the primary action, the focus ring, the active/selected state, and the live/running indicator, and little else. It is the brand signature precisely because it is scarce (well under 10% by visual weight). Do not tint large surfaces with it.
- **Teal = secondary / calm emphasis.** Good for secondary actions, links, informational state, and quiet highlights. It carries the brand without shouting.
- **Neutrals carry the app.** Surfaces, text, and borders are tinted near-neutrals, not pure gray.

### Palette & motion notes (apply when building)

- Use OKLCH. Tint neutrals toward the teal hue (~213) at very low chroma (about 0.004 to 0.012) so brand and UI cohere; reduce chroma further as lightness approaches the extremes. No pure `#000`/`#fff`, even in dark mode the darkest surface is an off-black with a hint of teal.
- When orange or teal sit on a near-black surface, drop their chroma slightly from the raw brand values so they read rich rather than glaring; keep orange clearly the more saturated of the two.
- 60/30/10 by visual weight: mostly surface/neutral, then secondary text/borders, then the rare orange accent reserved for the active/primary action and live state. Accents work because they are scarce.
- Status (running / idle / error / dead) must be distinguishable by shape, weight, or label, not hue alone (e-ink strips colour to monochrome). Map state to brand colour as enhancement only: e.g. running leans orange, but the running-ness must still read with all colour removed. Do not let "is this live?" depend on the orange.
- Avoid using brand colour for text on coloured fills; use a tint of the fill instead. Reserve solid orange/teal for marks, indicators, and small fills, not body copy.
- Motion is functional and minimal (transform/opacity only, exponential ease-out, no bounce) and already auto-disabled under e-ink. Favour one well-judged state transition over scattered micro-interactions.
